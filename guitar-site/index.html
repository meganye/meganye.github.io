Types of Guitars


Nylon-String Classical


Classical guitars, also known as nylon string or folk guitars, have hollow bodies and wider necks compared to steel string guitars. These guitars also have open pegheads, a stationary loop type bridge, and nylon strings tied at the base. Genres for this type include classical, folk, Latin, and jazz. Fingerpicking is the most common playing style used.



Steel-String Acoustic


With a flat top hollow body, pick guard, and a narrow neck, the steel string acoustic guitar is best suited for folk, country, ragtime, blues, and pop songs. It is recommended to use light gauge bronze strings with ball ends. Using a pick, fingers, or using the thumb and fingers.



Electric


There are two types of electric guitars: solid body and semi-hollow body. Both require amplification to be heard for regular playing. Sound relies heavily on pick-ups and the amplifier. The solid body electric is common used in blues and rock, while the semi-hollow body is used in country, rock, jazz, pop, and blues.


PARTS of a Guitar


by Fretjam


Sitting Position


The waist of the guitar should be plaed on the right thigh, with the neck at a 15 degree angle to the floor. Your forearm should rest on the guitar on top of the bridge base and never on your knee or leg. When playing, the wrist should arch slightly towards the floor.


Classical Position


A footstool should be used to elevate the left knee to a pointer higher than the hip. The spine needs to be kept straight and the guitar should be placed on the left thigh, with the neck at around a 45 degree angle from the ground.


Standing Position


A strap should be used to hold the guitar while standing. Straps are attatched to a pin on the end of a guitar and the head just above the nut. Most electric guitars have a strap button on the body.


Reading Music


Notes and Rests

The whole note represents 4 beats (count: 1,2,3,4), a half note gets 2 beats, and a quarter note gets 1 beat. Notes can get smaller, sometimes up to 1/64 or even smaller! A dotted note is 1.5 beats of the same note undotted. For example, a dotted half note is worth 3 beats.

There are also rest equivalents to notes.


Bars and Measures

Notes are divided into measures with bar lines. The double bar line signals the end of a song.


Treble Clef and Time Signature

At the beginning of the music is a clef and the time signature. Guitar sheet music uses the treble (G) clef, which tells us which notes each line and space will represent. The time signature is made up of two numbers, one on the top and one on the bottom. The top number shows how many beats are in one measure and the bottom number shows which note gets one beat.

In this case, there are 4 beats in one measure and a quarter note gets 1 beat.

The 4/4 time signature is the most common


Note Positions

There are notes on lines and spaces of the staff. Notes on spaces spell FACE and notes on lines are EGBDF (Every Good Boy Deserves Fudge)
